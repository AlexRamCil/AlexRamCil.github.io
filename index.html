1. Introduction¶

The Covid-19 pandemic has thrust many people into quarantines. After months of the pandemic, multiple vaccines have been developed to combat the virus. This tutorial will walk through an analysis of data presented by Johns Hopkins University. While observations of increasing vaccinations are easier to do, understanding trends of the data and comparing states to one another requires more data analysis. As such it is our goal to help explain the Johns Hopking Vaccination dataset as well as our methods utilized in our analysis.

3. Information About the Data¶

When conducting a thorough data analysis, the first thing that we want to do is find and obtain a dataset for us to manipulate. We ended up choosing Johns Hopkins Vaccination Data. This dataset contains information regarding vaccination data across each of the 50 U.S. states, as well as D.C., Puerto Rico, and various other U.S. territories.

As stated on the Johns Hopkins GitHub page: “This website and its contents herein, including all data, mapping, and analysis (“Website”), copyright 2020 Johns Hopkins University, all rights reserved, is provided to the public strictly for public health, educational, and academic research purposes. Redistribution of the Website or the aggregated data set underlying the Website is strictly prohibited. You are welcome to link to the Website, however.”

Thankfully for us, we intend to utilize this data for educational purposes, which means that we are able to access it freely. We appreciate Johns Hopkins for allowing us to work with this particular dataset, as we believe that analysis of the set will lead us to better understand issues regarding vaccination rates, the proportions of individuals vaccinated between states, etc.

4. Data Collection: Scraping¶

The data is stored as a .csv file on the Centers for Civic Impact GitHub page, under the "COVID-19" repository.

In order to obtain the data and manipulate it, we decided to store it into a Pandas DataFrame. For the purposes of this tutorial, we chose to drop some of the territories for different reasons. First off, we couldn't find any accurate information regarding the vaccination status of those that would be listed under "Department of Defense", "Indian Health Services", and any other government program. For the four territories that we did drop (American Samoa, Guam, Northern Mariana Islands, and the Virgin Islands), their information was not listed on the website below. We did not want to use a different website out of fear of the data being slightly off (outdated, different study conducted, etc.), so we chose to drop them from the table as well.

In the above plot, we can see the individual population proportion that has been vaccinated for each state (including D.C. and Puerto Rico). The five states with the highest proportion of their population vaccinated are Washington D.C., Puerto Rico, Rhode Island, Vermont and Connecticut. Although these states have the highest proportion of their population vaccinated, it’s important to note that three of the states (and territories) mentioned above are among those with the smallest population. Vermont, D.C., and Rhode Island are within the 10 least populated states in the U.S. Collectively, they only account for a little under 3% of the entire U.S. population (9,145,122 people). Just looking at each state’s individual vaccinated proportion from a static perspective doesn’t give us the information that we are looking for. To take our analysis a step further, we will take a look at the distribution of each state’s daily vaccinations over a 16-month period.

The above plot shows the proportion of Americans vaccinated over time from the start of the Covid-19 pandemic. This data can be analyzed and applied to approximately calculate how long it would take Americans to become vaccinated against a new strain or a new outbreak all together. According to the regression, 60 percent of Americans became vaccinated on approximately November 8th 2021.

The United States had its first confirmed case of Covid-19 on January 20th, 2020, meaning it took approximately 1 year and 10 months from the first confirmed case for 60 percent of U.S. citizens to become vaccinated against the virus. However, part of this time must be attributed to the development of the vaccine. Speaking of which, the FDA approved the first public vaccine on December 11th, 2020. This means the vaccine has been publicly available for close to 11 months. While many aspects of Covid-19 are unique and the development of a vaccine became the leading research in America, we can analyze the data to hypothesize that in the event of a new outbreak occurring it would take Americans, 11 months to reach 60 percent vaccination from the day the vaccine becomes available and 22 months from the first confirmed case in America.
